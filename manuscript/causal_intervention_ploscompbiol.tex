% ABSTRACT

\section*{Abstract}

We evaluate whether causal interventions on scGPT gene tokens recover known
transcription factor (TF)--target dependencies beyond attention-only heuristics.
Using Tabula Sapiens kidney, lung, and immune subsets plus an external lung
atlas, we ablate or swap TF token values and quantify changes in target-token
readouts. We compare intervention scores to curated gene regulatory references
(TRRUST, DoRothEA), include attention and coexpression baselines, trace
component-level circuits via activation patching, and validate on CRISPR
perturbation data from Adamson. To quantitatively position the approach
against prior work, we also benchmark it against eleven classical GRN
inference baselines spanning six algorithmic families (Pearson, Spearman,
and distance correlation; partial correlation; mutual information, CLR,
and ARACNE; LASSO and elastic net; GRNBoost2 and GENIE3) on a matched
cell sample, matched preprocessing, and the same TRRUST pair sampling
protocol.
Kidney multi-seed runs show modest enrichment (AUPR~$\approx$~0.60), while
lung results are substantially stronger (AUPR up to 0.78, permutation
$p{=}0.001$) and remain positive in higher-pair settings. On the matched
benchmark, scGPT leads the strongest classical baseline by +0.053 in
kidney, +0.075 in lung, and +0.002 in immune, consistent with the
tissue-level pattern observed in the main runs. Mechanistic tracing
localizes effects to a small set of attention heads and mid-to-late MLP
blocks. External perturbation validation remains low power but provides
an independent check of causal signals. We present the full pipeline,
quantitative evidence, and an honest assessment of current limitations.
Source code and evaluation artifacts are available at
\url{https://github.com/Biodyn-AI/casual-intervention}.

\section*{Author summary}

We wanted to understand whether a large AI model trained on gene expression
data from millions of human cells has actually learned how genes regulate each
other, or whether it has only memorized statistical patterns. To test this, we
deliberately disrupted one gene's representation inside the model and measured
how much this changed the model's predictions for known regulatory targets.
We found that the answer depends on the tissue. In lung tissue, the model
appears to have learned genuine regulatory relationships that go beyond simple
correlations, with disruptions to transcription factor genes causing
significant changes in their known targets. In kidney and immune tissues, the
signal was weaker. We also traced which internal components carry these
regulatory signals, finding that a small number of attention heads and neural
network layers are responsible. Our work provides a practical toolkit for
auditing whether biological AI models have learned real biology or just
correlations, which matters as these models are increasingly used for drug
target discovery and cell type classification.

\section*{Introduction}

Transformer-based \cite{vaswani_attention_2017} single-cell foundation models,
including scGPT \cite{cui_scgpt_2023}, Geneformer
\cite{theodoris_geneformer_2023}, scBERT \cite{yang_scbert_2022}, and
scFoundation \cite{hao_scfoundation_2024}, capture gene dependencies at
scale, but attention weights alone are correlational and do not establish
causal influence in the sense of Pearl's interventionist framework
\cite{pearl_causality_2009}. Prior gene regulatory network (GRN) inference methods capture TF--target
relationships through diverse computational strategies that span two decades
of algorithmic development. Information-theoretic approaches such as ARACNE
\cite{margolin_aracne_2006} use mutual information with data processing
inequality pruning, and CLR \cite{faith_clr_2007} extends relevance networks
with context-dependent likelihood scoring; both were originally developed for
bulk expression data and later adapted to single-cell contexts. Tree-based
regression methods, notably GENIE3 \cite{huynhthu_genie3_2010} and its
scalable successor GRNBoost2 \cite{moerman_grnboost2_2019}, predict each
target gene's expression from the expression of candidate TFs using random
forests or gradient boosting, offering nonlinear modeling without requiring
explicit network topology. SCENIC \cite{aibar_scenic_2017} augments GRNBoost2
with cis-regulatory motif enrichment to prune indirect edges, and its scalable
Python implementation pySCENIC \cite{vandesande_pyscenic_2020} has become one
of the most widely adopted GRN pipelines. The multimodal extension SCENIC+
\cite{bravo_scenicplus_2023} further integrates single-cell chromatin
accessibility with expression data, jointly inferring enhancer--TF--gene
relationships. The Inferelator \cite{bonneau_grn_review_2007}, originally
designed for perturbation time-series with regularized regression, has been
updated in its third iteration \cite{gibbs_inferelator3_2022} to handle
large-scale single-cell data with multi-task learning across cell types.
Temporal and pseudotemporal methods offer a distinct perspective:
PIDC \cite{chan_pidc_2017} uses partial information decomposition to
distinguish direct from indirect regulatory interactions in single-cell data,
SINGE \cite{deshpande_singe_2022} applies kernel-based Granger causality
ensembles along pseudotemporal orderings, and Dynamo \cite{qiu_dynamo_2022}
reconstructs continuous transcriptomic vector fields from RNA velocity,
enabling causal analysis of regulatory dynamics. CellOracle
\cite{kamimoto_celloracle_2023} combines chromatin accessibility with
expression data to enable in silico perturbation of inferred networks, directly
linking regulatory topology to predicted cell-state transitions. All of these
methods operate on expression, accessibility, or velocity data measured from
cells, whereas foundation-model GRNs emerge as a byproduct of self-supervised
pretraining on expression reconstruction, raising the question of whether the
resulting representations encode genuine regulatory structure or merely
statistical co-occurrence patterns.

Systematic GRN benchmarks have provided increasingly rigorous standards for
evaluating inference methods. The DREAM5 challenge \cite{marbach_dream5_2012}
established the principle that community-consensus networks outperform
individual methods, evaluated across \textit{E.~coli}, \textit{S.~aureus},
and \textit{S.~cerevisiae} using both in silico benchmarks from GeneNetWeaver
\cite{schaffter_genenetweaver_2011} and experimentally validated edges.
BEELINE \cite{pratapa_beeline_2020} introduced a containerized framework for
benchmarking twelve single-cell GRN methods on both synthetic (from the
SERGIO simulator \cite{dibaeinia_sergio_2020}, which generates realistic
single-cell expression guided by known network topologies) and curated
datasets, revealing that no method dominates across all settings. More recent
benchmarks have extended this work: Karamveer and Uzun
\cite{karamveer_benchmark_2024} evaluated methods on both simulated and
experimental single-cell datasets with attention to practical factors like
runtime and scalability, while STREAMLINE \cite{stock_streamline_2024}
introduced topological benchmarking criteria that go beyond simple edge
recovery to assess whether inferred networks capture biologically meaningful
structural properties such as degree distributions and community structure.
Despite this rigorous landscape, none of these benchmarks tests whether a
model's \textit{internal representations} encode directional TF--target
relationships that survive causal intervention, because the methods under
evaluation extract regulatory structure from data rather than learning it
implicitly through pretraining.

Evaluation of single-cell foundation models has focused primarily on downstream
task performance: cell-type annotation accuracy, batch integration quality,
perturbation response prediction, and gene embedding similarity
\cite{cui_scgpt_2023,theodoris_geneformer_2023,hao_scfoundation_2024}.
Perturbation prediction has received particular attention, with GEARS
\cite{roohani_gears_2024} combining graph neural networks with gene
interaction knowledge to predict multigene perturbation outcomes, setting a
benchmark against which foundation model perturbation capabilities are
increasingly measured. Tabular baselines like scTab
\cite{fischer_sctab_2024}, trained on 22 million cells with standard
nonlinear architectures, have demonstrated competitive or superior
performance to foundation models on cell-type annotation tasks, raising
questions about whether the representational richness of transformer-based
models translates to practical advantage. Recent zero-shot evaluations
\cite{kedzierska_zeroshot_2025} have probed whether foundation models
generalize beyond their training distribution, revealing that scGPT and
Geneformer underperform simpler embedding baselines (including scVI and PCA)
in zero-shot settings, which suggests that the models' strong benchmark
performance may partly reflect in-distribution memorization rather than
transferable biological understanding. DoRothEA-based activity inference
through tools like decoupleR \cite{badia_decoupler_2022} provides one external
reference for regulatory relationships, but this evaluates inferred activities
rather than the model's internal representations.

Mechanistic interpretability offers a fundamentally different evaluation
paradigm. In NLP, causal mediation analysis \cite{vig_causal_mediation_2020}
and activation patching \cite{meng_rome_2022,goldowsky_dill_path_patching_2023}
have been used to localize factual knowledge and algorithmic circuits within
transformer internals, while the circuits framework
\cite{olah_zoom_in_2020,elhage_framework_2021} and automated circuit
discovery \cite{conmy_acdc_2023} provide systematic tools for decomposing
model behavior. Landmark case studies, such as the indirect object
identification circuit in GPT-2 \cite{wang_ioi_2023}, have shown that complex
behaviors can be attributed to specific sets of attention heads organized into
functional groups. The theoretical foundations for this work draw on causal
abstraction \cite{geiger_causal_abstraction_2021}, which formalizes when a
high-level causal model faithfully describes the computations of a neural
network, and causal scrubbing \cite{chan_causal_scrubbing_2022}, which provides
a complementary framework for testing circuit-level hypotheses. In
computational biology, analogous interpretability work has emerged for protein
language models: Vig et al.\ \cite{vig_bertology_biology_2021} showed that
attention heads in protein transformers capture secondary structure, binding
sites, and contact maps with increasing complexity across layers, while the
ESM family \cite{rives_esm_2021,lin_esmfold_2023} demonstrated that
unsupervised pretraining on protein sequences produces representations from
which three-dimensional structure can be predicted. However, no prior work
has applied causal intervention or activation patching to single-cell
foundation models to test whether their internal representations encode
mechanistically interpretable gene regulatory circuits. Our work fills this
gap by adapting the NLP interpretability toolkit to the biological token
structure of scGPT, providing a complementary evaluation axis that tests not
just whether outputs correlate with known regulation, but whether the internal
computation is causally organized around TF--target dependencies.

We ask whether direct causal interventions on scGPT token
representations produce scores aligned with curated TF--target edges, and
whether mechanistic tracing can localize the internal components responsible
for those effects.

Our approach draws on causal mediation analysis
\cite{vig_causal_mediation_2020} and activation patching techniques developed
for language model interpretability
\cite{meng_rome_2022,goldowsky_dill_path_patching_2023}, adapting them to the
biological token structure of single-cell transformers. The mechanistic
interpretability paradigm \cite{olah_zoom_in_2020} seeks to decompose neural
network computations into understandable circuits, and recent automated methods
\cite{conmy_acdc_2023} have begun to scale this approach. Superposition
\cite{elhage_superposition_2022} poses a challenge for such analyses, as
features may share dimensions, but the relatively sparse gene-token structure
of scGPT may partially mitigate this concern. Related formalisms include causal
scrubbing \cite{chan_causal_scrubbing_2022}, which provides a complementary
framework for testing circuit-level hypotheses.

Our evaluation focuses on two complementary signals: curated regulatory
interactions (TRRUST and DoRothEA) and perturbation-derived edges from CRISPR
screening data \cite{dixit_perturbseq_2016,datlinger_perturbseq_2017}. The
growing availability of large-scale perturbation atlases
\cite{replogle_perturbseq_2022,norman_crispr_2019} provides increasingly rich
ground truth for such evaluations. The first signal provides a broad reference
derived from literature curation, while the second provides experimentally
induced causal signals. By aligning causal intervention scores with both, we
probe whether internal gene-token dependencies show evidence of causal
structure beyond correlation.

We introduce a causal intervention pipeline for scGPT that provides
cross-tissue and multi-seed results, compares against attention and
coexpression baselines as well as eleven classical GRN inference methods
evaluated on a matched substrate, localizes circuitry with activation
patching, and reports a perturbation-based validation using Adamson
CRISPR data.

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Kidney multi-seed results}

Across four kidney seeds, TRRUST ablation achieves mean AUPR 0.6018 with
bootstrap 95\% CI [0.575, 0.628], while DoRothEA ablation reaches 0.5369
[0.505, 0.563]. Swap interventions are weaker (TRRUST 0.537, DoRothEA 0.509).
Evidence coverage is consistent (165--175 pairs from 240 sampled), stabilizing
estimates. The seed-level spread is modest (0.57--0.63 for TRRUST ablation),
indicating reproducibility at current scale (Fig~\ref{fig:kidney-seed-ci}).

The fact that ablation consistently outperforms swap deserves attention: zeroing
out a TF token value removes that gene's contribution entirely, producing a
clean counterfactual, whereas swapping replaces it with another gene's value,
which may partially preserve the original regulatory signal if the replacement
gene participates in similar pathways. In other words, ablation tests
\textit{necessity} of the TF representation, while swap is a noisier probe that
conflates removal with substitution. The gap between the two suggests that
scGPT encodes at least some TF-specific information that cannot be replaced by
an arbitrary gene token, which is a prerequisite for genuine causal structure
rather than generic positional or cell-state effects.

The modest absolute AUPR values (around 0.60 against a random baseline of
$\sim$0.50 in balanced settings) reflect the inherent difficulty of
recovering directed regulatory edges from a model that was trained on
expression reconstruction, not on regulatory inference per se. Even a
partially enriched signal is informative because it suggests that the model's
internal representations are not agnostic to regulatory relationships.

\subsection*{Cross-tissue performance}

Lung shows substantially stronger enrichment than kidney or immune
(Table~\ref{tab:cross-tissue}). Lung TRRUST ablation AUPR reaches 0.7272
(permutation $p{=}0.001$) and DoRothEA 0.7820 ($p{=}0.001$). Immune results are
weaker and nonsignificant, while the Krasnow lung dataset shows mixed
alignment consistent with dataset shift. The high-pair lung run (160+160)
retains strong performance (TRRUST 0.7574, $p{=}0.004$), indicating robustness
to expanded candidate sets (Fig~\ref{fig:cross-tissue}).

The tissue-level variation has a plausible biological explanation. Lung tissue
in Tabula Sapiens contains well-characterized epithelial and endothelial cell
types with active, tissue-specific transcription factor programs (e.g., NKX2-1,
FOXJ1, SOX family members) that are richly represented in curated databases.
Kidney has a more heterogeneous cell composition with many transporter and
metabolic genes that may be less well-covered by TF-centric references like
TRRUST. Immune cells, while transcriptionally active, often rely on
combinatorial and context-dependent regulation (e.g., cytokine signaling
cascades) that single-TF ablation may not capture cleanly.

The Krasnow lung dataset, collected with Smart-seq2 rather than 10x Chromium,
shows lower TRRUST alignment (AUPR 0.36) despite being lung tissue. This
likely reflects platform effects on the expression distributions that scGPT was
pretrained on: the model's whole-human checkpoint was trained predominantly on
droplet-based data, and the distributional shift from Smart-seq2 full-length
transcripts may degrade the learned TF representations. The fact that DoRothEA
alignment remains moderately positive (0.63) on the same dataset suggests that
some regulatory signals survive the platform shift, particularly the
higher-confidence interactions captured by DoRothEA's literature scoring.

\subsection*{Baselines versus causal scores}

Causal scores outperform attention and coexpression baselines for lung and
high-pair lung runs, while kidney and immune are competitive with baselines
(Table~\ref{tab:baseline}). The lung improvement is substantial: causal AUPR
exceeds attention by +0.184 and coexpression by +0.140. Kidney gains are
small (+0.017), and immune shows negative deltas. Reduced-gene runs reinforce
this: reduced lung remains above attention (0.674 vs.\ 0.608, $p{=}0.007$),
while reduced kidney trails attention (Fig~\ref{fig:baseline}).

The relationship between these three scoring methods reveals something
important about what the model has learned. Attention scores capture which
tokens the model ``looks at'' during forward computation, but high attention
does not imply causal influence because attention heads can attend to tokens
for context without propagating regulatory information. Coexpression
correlations capture statistical co-occurrence of gene pairs across cells,
which can arise from shared upstream regulation, common cell-state programs,
or true direct regulation. Causal intervention scores, by contrast, test
whether \textit{removing} a specific TF token changes the model's prediction
at the target position, which is a more direct test of functional dependency
within the model's learned representation.

The fact that causal scores exceed both baselines in lung but not in kidney or
immune suggests that the model has learned genuinely directional TF--target
relationships for lung-specific programs, whereas in kidney and immune tissues,
the model's gene representations may be dominated by broader cell-state
signatures that correlational methods capture equally well. This is consistent
with the intuition that causal interventions are most informative when the
underlying signal has clear directionality, rather than being a diffuse
cell-type marker.

\subsection*{Comparison with classical GRN inference methods}

To place the causal intervention scores alongside established gene regulatory
network (GRN) inference methods, we ran eleven classical baselines spanning
six algorithmic families on a \emph{matched} reproduction of the main
runs' tissue substrates (Table~\ref{tab:grn-baselines}). For every tissue
we started from the same raw Tabula Sapiens h5ad, re-applied the
preprocessing pipeline (normalize\_total~=~10\,000, log1p, 5\,000 highly
variable genes restricted to the scGPT vocabulary), drew a 120-cell sample
with seed 42 matching \texttt{max\_cells=120} in the main config files,
reproduced the deterministic 120 positive + 120 random negative TRRUST
pair sampling from \texttt{run\_causal\_interventions.py}, and applied the
same evidence filter (each pair is kept when both source and target
co-occur in the top-1200 expressed genes of at least one cell, matching
the scGPT dataset's \texttt{max\_genes=1200} token selection). Because
HVG selection is variance-dependent and the original main-run processed
h5ad files for lung and immune are no longer cached, the baseline
substrate is a bit-for-bit reproduction of the preprocessing protocol
rather than of the exact gene set used by the original main runs; the
evaluation pair distribution differs from the main runs' by a few
percent, which is why the resulting Pearson coexpression numbers in
Table~\ref{tab:grn-baselines} differ slightly from the coexpression
column of Table~\ref{tab:baseline}. On this matched substrate we
evaluated:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item \textit{Coexpression family.}
      (i)~Pearson correlation;
      (ii)~Spearman rank correlation;
      (iii)~distance correlation \cite{szekely_distance_correlation_2007},
      a nonlinear measure of statistical dependence.
\item \textit{Graphical model family.}
      (iv)~Partial correlation on a ridge-regularized precision matrix
      \cite{pearl_causality_2009}, representing Gaussian graphical models
      and the PC-algorithm lineage.
\item \textit{Information-theoretic family.}
      (v)~Pairwise mutual information;
      (vi)~CLR \cite{faith_clr_2007}, applying a per-row / per-column
      z-score normalization on top of the MI matrix;
      (vii)~ARACNE \cite{margolin_aracne_2006}, applying the data
      processing inequality to prune indirect edges.
\item \textit{Regularized regression family.}
      (viii)~LASSO regression \cite{tibshirani_lasso_1996};
      (ix)~elastic net regression \cite{zou_elasticnet_2005},
      representing the Inferelator lineage
      \cite{bonneau_grn_review_2007,gibbs_inferelator3_2022}.
\item \textit{Tree-based regression family.}
      (x)~GRNBoost2 \cite{moerman_grnboost2_2019} with per-target
      stochastic gradient boosting;
      (xi)~GENIE3 \cite{huynhthu_genie3_2010} with per-target random
      forests. Both methods score edges by the feature importance of
      the source TF in the regressor that predicts the target's
      expression, following the original papers.
\end{itemize}

All eleven baselines are implemented with standard scientific Python
libraries (scikit-learn, SciPy, \texttt{dcor}) so that the comparison is
self-contained and does not depend on any unmaintained dask cluster
backends. AUPR, AUROC, and permutation $p$-values (1\,000 label shuffles)
are computed with the same evaluation function used for the causal
scores.

Across all three tissues the scGPT causal ablation scores remain ahead of
every classical GRN method (Table~\ref{tab:grn-baselines},
Fig~\ref{fig:grn-baseline}). The gap varies in a biologically interpretable
way. In lung, the causal AUPR of 0.727 exceeds the strongest baseline
(partial correlation, 0.652, permutation $p{=}0.001$) by +0.075, and every
other baseline by $\geq$+0.18; lung is also the tissue where the paper's
permutation-significant enrichment is most stable
(Table~\ref{tab:cross-tissue}), and the fact that a GGM-style de-confounded
coexpression is the closest competitor is consistent with the interpretation
that lung epithelial regulatory programs leave clear linear signatures in
expression. In kidney, scGPT leads the best classical method (Spearman
correlation, 0.549) by +0.053; among the tree-based and information-theoretic
families only CLR (0.502) and ARACNE (0.505) are nominally above the
balanced-class chance level of 0.50, while mutual information, GRNBoost2,
and GENIE3 sit below it. In immune, scGPT (0.596) is essentially tied with
Spearman correlation (0.594) and the regularized regressions (LASSO 0.537
with $p{=}0.019$, elastic net 0.552 with $p{=}0.012$) also reach nominal
significance; this is the same tissue in which
Table~\ref{tab:cross-tissue} already reports scGPT at $p{=}0.359$, so the
quantitative comparison reinforces the paper's claim that immune
regulatory signal is not recovered preferentially by the foundation model.

Several conclusions follow. First, the foundation model provides a real
advantage over classical methods where the paper's own cross-tissue analysis
said it would (lung) and essentially no advantage where the paper already
reported it to be weak (immune), with kidney sitting between. Second,
the strongest classical baseline is \textit{not} the most computationally
elaborate one: partial correlation and Spearman rank correlation
consistently beat tree-based and MI-based methods at this cell budget.
This is consistent with BEELINE \cite{pratapa_beeline_2020}, which shows
that tree-based and information-theoretic GRN inference typically requires
many hundreds to thousands of cells to converge on direct edges; 120 cells
is simply not enough observations for those methods. Third, the comparison
therefore speaks to signal efficiency at modest cell budgets rather than
to large-scale absolute accuracy, which the existing GRN benchmarking
literature \cite{marbach_dream5_2012,pratapa_beeline_2020,stock_streamline_2024}
already documents in depth on dedicated datasets.

\subsection*{Mechanistic case studies}

Activation patching localizes specific TF--target effects to a small set of
attention heads and MLP blocks. Example pairs
(GATA3${\rightarrow}$GATA3, FOS${\rightarrow}$FOS,
JUN${\rightarrow}$JUN, JUN${\rightarrow}$TNF) consistently highlight
mid-to-late MLPs and a handful of heads (Fig~\ref{fig:case-study},
Table~\ref{tab:case-studies}). Self-targeting pairs include late MLP blocks
(L8--L11), whereas cross-gene JUN${\rightarrow}$TNF concentrates on earlier
blocks (L0--L5), suggesting different interaction types engage distinct depth
regimes. Head lists are dominated by H0/H1 across layers L0--L7, indicating a
small subset carries most restoration signal.

The depth separation between self-targeting and cross-gene interactions aligns
with a natural hypothesis about how the model processes gene-gene
relationships. Self-targeting effects (a TF influencing its own output token)
can be resolved through relatively local, residual-stream computations that
accumulate through later MLP blocks, since the source and target share the same
token position. Cross-gene effects like JUN${\rightarrow}$TNF require
inter-token communication through attention, which must happen earlier in the
network so that downstream MLP blocks can integrate the transferred
information. The observation that cross-gene pairs rely on earlier components
is consistent with transformer architectures where attention moves information
between positions and MLPs transform it within positions.

These circuit-level findings, while based on a small set of case-study pairs,
provide a template for future mechanistic audits. If specific heads or MLP
blocks consistently mediate known regulatory effects, they could serve as
targets for surgical interventions to test whether the model's regulatory
knowledge is localized or distributed, a question with practical implications
for model editing and fine-tuning strategies in biological foundation models.

\subsection*{Perturbation validation}

Adamson perturbation validation remains low power at current scale. The scaled
run (40+40 pairs) yields AUPR 0.435 for ablation and 0.502 for swap; the
large run (100+100 pairs) yields 0.498 and 0.500. None are significant under
permutation tests (Table~\ref{tab:perturb}). As pair counts increase, AUPR
drifts toward chance, which we believe reflects several compounding factors
rather than a single failure mode.

First, Adamson perturbation-derived edges are defined by the top-$k$ expression
changes after CRISPR knockout, which captures the strongest downstream
\textit{transcriptional} effects. However, scGPT's causal intervention operates
at the \textit{representational} level, testing whether removing a gene token's
value changes the model's output at another token position. These are
conceptually related but not identical: a gene that is a strong transcriptional
target of a TF in real cells may not be the gene whose model representation is
most disrupted by ablating the TF token, especially if the model has learned to
represent the target through cell-state features rather than direct
TF-mediated effects.

Second, the Adamson dataset uses a CRISPRi screen in K562 cells (a chronic
myelogenous leukemia line), which is biologically distant from the primary
tissues on which scGPT was pretrained. The model may encode regulatory
relationships specific to normal tissue contexts that do not align with
K562-specific perturbation effects.

Third, the evidence filter (requiring both TF and target to co-occur in the
scGPT evaluation cells) substantially reduces the effective evaluation set,
compressing statistical power. At 75--175 evidence pairs with near-chance AUPR,
the permutation test lacks the resolution to detect a weak true signal even if
one exists.

relationships rather than spurious correlations with loosely curated edges.

\section*{Discussion}

Our results paint a nuanced picture of what single-cell foundation models
learn about gene regulation. Causal ablations yield modest enrichment on kidney
and substantially stronger performance on lung, with significant TRRUST
enrichment across multiple lung configurations. This pattern is consistent
across reference databases, robustness runs, and high-confidence subsets,
which makes it unlikely to be an artifact of a single evaluation choice.

The tissue dependence of causal signal strength carries an important
implication for how we think about foundation model representations. Rather
than encoding a universal regulatory program, scGPT appears to have learned
tissue-specific regulatory structure that is stronger where training data
provided richer and more coherent transcriptional programs. Lung, with its
well-characterized epithelial differentiation hierarchies, may offer cleaner
TF-target signals for the model to internalize during pretraining, whereas
kidney and immune tissues present more complex or combinatorial regulatory
logic that a token-level ablation may not isolate cleanly.

Placing these results in the context of existing GRN inference approaches, our
causal intervention framework addresses a fundamentally different question from
the one posed by classical methods. Tree-based approaches such as GENIE3
\cite{huynhthu_genie3_2010} and GRNBoost2 \cite{moerman_grnboost2_2019} ask
``can we predict a target gene's expression from TF expression profiles?'',
while information-theoretic methods like ARACNE \cite{margolin_aracne_2006}
and CLR \cite{faith_clr_2007} ask ``do TF and target share mutual information
beyond what is explained by indirect paths?''. SCENIC \cite{aibar_scenic_2017}
and its multimodal successor SCENIC+ \cite{bravo_scenicplus_2023} combine
expression-based edge prediction with cis-regulatory motif enrichment or
chromatin accessibility data to prune false positives. Temporal methods such
as SINGE \cite{deshpande_singe_2022} leverage pseudotime ordering to infer
directional Granger-causal relationships, while CellOracle
\cite{kamimoto_celloracle_2023} uses inferred networks for in silico
perturbation of cell states. All of these methods test whether regulatory
structure can be \textit{extracted} from measured data, and systematic
benchmarks from the DREAM5 challenge \cite{marbach_dream5_2012} through
BEELINE \cite{pratapa_beeline_2020} and STREAMLINE
\cite{stock_streamline_2024} have evaluated them on edge recovery, topological
fidelity, and scalability. Our approach poses the complementary question: has
a pretrained foundation model \textit{internalized} regulatory structure in
its learned representations, such that ablating a TF's representation causally
disrupts the model's computation at known target positions? The two evaluation
paradigms are not interchangeable. A model might produce outputs that correlate
with known regulation (as captured by standard downstream evaluation) without
encoding causally organized TF--target dependencies in its internal
computation, or it might encode genuine causal structure that standard
output-level evaluations miss entirely.

To anchor this distinction empirically, we ran eleven classical GRN
baselines spanning six algorithmic families --- Pearson, Spearman, and
distance correlation (coexpression); partial correlation (Gaussian
graphical models); pairwise mutual information, CLR, and ARACNE
(information-theoretic); LASSO and elastic net regression (the
Inferelator lineage); and GRNBoost2 and GENIE3 (tree-based regression) ---
on a matched reproduction of the paper's main kidney, lung, and immune
substrates, using the same preprocessing recipe, 120-cell sampling with
seed 42, deterministic TRRUST pair sampling from the same
\texttt{\_build\_pair\_list} logic, and the same top-1200 evidence
filter (Table~\ref{tab:grn-baselines}, Fig~\ref{fig:grn-baseline}). With this
matched substrate, scGPT's causal ablation AUPR leads the \emph{strongest}
classical method by +0.053 in kidney (vs.\ Spearman correlation, 0.549),
+0.075 in lung (vs.\ ridge-regularized partial correlation, 0.652, whose
permutation-significant $p{=}0.001$ is itself notable), and only +0.002 in
immune (vs.\ Spearman correlation, 0.594). The ordering mirrors the
tissue-level pattern the paper already reports from the main runs: the
lung gap is large and significant, the kidney gap is smaller and in line
with the paper's modest kidney enrichment, and the immune comparison is
effectively a tie, matching the paper's observation that immune causal
scores sit close to attention and coexpression baselines.

Two features of this comparison are worth highlighting. First, the
strongest classical competitors are not the most elaborate methods.
Partial correlation on a ridge-regularized precision matrix is the closest
competitor in lung, and Spearman rank correlation is the closest in kidney
and immune. Tree-based and information-theoretic methods --- GRNBoost2,
GENIE3, mutual information, CLR, ARACNE --- sit near chance in every
tissue, consistent with BEELINE \cite{pratapa_beeline_2020}, which shows
that these methods typically require many hundreds to thousands of cells
to converge on direct edges. At 120 cells they simply do not have enough
observations, while the correlation-based methods recover part of the
signal from the same budget. Second, the comparison therefore speaks to
signal \textit{efficiency} at modest cell counts rather than to absolute
accuracy at large scale, which existing benchmarks already cover in depth
\cite{marbach_dream5_2012,pratapa_beeline_2020,stock_streamline_2024}. A
pretrained foundation model that has already absorbed tens of millions of
cells can provide directional regulatory evidence from a small held-out
sample where classical methods do not yet have enough observations to do
so, while the classical methods remain the better choice when large cell
numbers are available and the goal is explicit network construction rather
than interrogation of a pretrained model's internal representations.

The relationship between our work and prior foundation model evaluations is
similarly one of complementarity rather than competition. Task-based benchmarks
\cite{cui_scgpt_2023,theodoris_geneformer_2023,hao_scfoundation_2024} treat
the model as a black box and measure whether fine-tuned outputs match expected
labels for cell-type annotation, batch integration, or perturbation response
prediction; GEARS \cite{roohani_gears_2024} extends this to combinatorial
perturbation prediction using graph-augmented architectures. Tabular baselines
\cite{fischer_sctab_2024} have shown that simpler models trained on comparable
data can match or exceed foundation model performance on specific annotation
tasks, questioning whether the transformer architecture itself contributes
meaningful biological insight beyond what scale and data provide. Zero-shot
evaluations \cite{kedzierska_zeroshot_2025} have further revealed that
foundation models underperform simpler embeddings in out-of-distribution
settings, suggesting that strong benchmark numbers may partly reflect
memorization of training-distribution statistics. Our mechanistic approach
probes a dimension that none of these evaluations address: whether the model's
internal computation is organized around biologically meaningful regulatory
circuits, and if so, which specific transformer components carry that
information under which tissue conditions. The fact that we observe strong
causal enrichment in lung tissue but not in immune tissue suggests that the
answer is conditional on the biological context, a finding that would be
invisible to output-only evaluation frameworks.

Our circuit-level analysis connects to the broader mechanistic interpretability
program that has proven productive for NLP transformers
\cite{olah_zoom_in_2020,elhage_framework_2021,wang_ioi_2023}. In those
settings, researchers have decomposed complex behaviors into interpretable
circuits of attention heads and MLP blocks, localized factual knowledge to
specific layers \cite{meng_rome_2022}, and developed formal frameworks for
testing whether neural computations faithfully implement hypothesized causal
models \cite{geiger_causal_abstraction_2021}. In computational biology,
analogous interpretability efforts have shown that protein language model
attention heads capture secondary structure, binding sites, and contact maps
\cite{vig_bertology_biology_2021}, and that unsupervised protein sequence
training produces representations sufficient for structure prediction
\cite{rives_esm_2021,lin_esmfold_2023}. Our activation patching results
provide initial evidence that a similar mechanistic decomposition may be
tractable for single-cell transformers: the observation that cross-gene
regulatory effects (e.g., JUN${\rightarrow}$TNF) concentrate on earlier
transformer layers than self-targeting effects suggests a functional
organization where attention-mediated inter-token communication precedes
MLP-mediated within-token processing, consistent with the general transformer
circuit framework \cite{elhage_framework_2021}.

The baseline comparisons add an important calibration. In tissues where causal
scores do not clearly exceed attention or coexpression, the intervention
pipeline is not providing additional information beyond what simple
correlational proxies capture. This does not invalidate the approach, but it
does suggest that causal interventions are most valuable in a targeted
setting: when there is reason to believe a specific tissue or TF program is
well-represented in the model, intervention-based scoring can extract
directional information that correlational methods miss.

The concentration of restoration effects in a small set of heads and MLP
blocks suggests a tractable mechanistic substrate within scGPT, offering
targets for focused audits and hypothesis-driven validation. If future work
confirms that specific heads or MLP blocks consistently mediate known
regulatory effects across larger sets of TF--target pairs, they could serve
as targets for surgical model editing or fine-tuning strategies, analogous to
how ROME \cite{meng_rome_2022} enables targeted knowledge editing in NLP
transformers by localizing factual associations to specific layers.

Perturbation validation results remain inconclusive, which we attribute
primarily to the biological distance between the K562 CRISPR screen and the
normal-tissue contexts on which the model was evaluated, compounded by limited
statistical power from evidence filtering. Future work should prioritize
perturbation datasets from primary tissues matching the evaluation context,
such as Perturb-seq in lung epithelial cells, to provide a fairer test of
whether the model's causal signals align with experimentally induced effects.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our evaluation has several limitations that scope the conclusions. All runs
use CPU-limited subsets (30--120 cells per run) with moderate pair counts
(30--160 positive pairs), which limits statistical power particularly for the
perturbation validation where only 75--175 evidence pairs are available. The
readout metric is a single target-token scalar (mean across hidden features),
which may not fully capture the dimensionality of regulatory effects encoded
in the model's representations. The perturbation validation relies on the
Adamson dataset, which uses a CRISPRi screen in K562 cells that are
biologically distant from the primary tissues used in the main evaluation;
this mismatch likely contributes to the inconclusive perturbation results.
Gene symbol mapping from Ensembl identifiers to HGNC symbols can introduce
alias errors, potentially misaligning a small fraction of evaluation pairs.
The mechanistic case studies cover only four TF--target pairs and may not
generalize to the full range of regulatory interactions. Finally, our
evaluation uses a single foundation model (scGPT); extending to other
architectures is needed to determine whether the observed patterns are
model-specific or reflect general properties of expression-trained
transformers.

\section*{Materials and methods}

\subsection*{Model and datasets}

We use the scGPT whole-human checkpoint from the model zoo, loaded with
standard model arguments. Fast transformer backends are disabled for
deterministic patching. All runs are CPU-only with fixed random seeds.

We use Tabula Sapiens kidney, lung, and immune subsets
\cite{tabula_sapiens_2022}, an external lung atlas from
Travaglini et al.\ \cite{travaglini_lung_2020}, and the Adamson CRISPR
perturbation dataset \cite{adamson_dataverse_2022}. All datasets were
originally generated using droplet-based protocols
\cite{zheng_10x_2017} except the Krasnow lung atlas (Smart-seq2).
Preprocessing follows standard Scanpy \cite{wolf_scanpy_2018} workflows
(normalize\_total=10\,000, log1p), selects 5\,000 highly variable genes
restricted to the scGPT vocabulary, and normalizes gene symbols via HGNC alias
mappings. We note that deep learning denoising approaches
\cite{eraslan_sc_review_2019} and integration methods
\cite{stuart_seurat_2019} were not applied, as we sought to evaluate the
foundation model's representations on minimally processed data.

\subsection*{Intervention pairs and scoring}

Positive labels come from curated TF--target edges in TRRUST \cite{han_trrust_2015}
and DoRothEA \cite{dorothea_github}; negatives are random controls from the same gene sets.
For each TF--target pair and each cell containing both genes, we compute a baseline readout and an
intervened readout at the target token position, reduced by mean across
features. We apply value ablation (TF token value zeroed) and token-value swap
interventions. The pair score is the mean absolute effect
$|\bar{\Delta}| = |y_{\text{baseline}} - y_{\text{intervened}}|$ across cells.

\subsection*{Causal tracing}

We patch clean activations into ablated forward passes and measure restoration
of the target readout, yielding per-layer, per-head, and per-MLP restoration
scores for selected case-study pairs.

\subsection*{Baselines and metrics}

We compare causal scores against (i)~attention scores aggregated across all
layers and heads and (ii)~coexpression correlations computed from normalized
expression. We compute AUPR and AUROC \cite{davis_prauc_2006} on pairs with
evidence; significance is
estimated by 1\,000 permutations of the labels, reporting the fraction of
permutations with AUPR $\geq$ observed (median across seeds where applicable).
We additionally report DoRothEA A/B subsets for high-confidence evaluation.

\subsection*{Perturbation validation}

We derive perturbation edges from Adamson \cite{adamson_cell_2016,adamson_dataverse_2022}
by selecting the top-$k$ ($k{=}50$) absolute expression deltas versus control per perturbation.
We evaluate causal scores on balanced pair sets: 40+40 (scaled) and 100+100 (large), with
max\_cells=60 per run.

\subsection*{Experimental configurations}

We evaluate kidney (four seeds), lung, and immune subsets with 120 max cells
and 120+120 candidate pairs. Robustness runs include a high-pair lung setting
(160+160 pairs), reduced-gene runs, and a Krasnow lung dataset. Adamson
experiments use scaled (40+40) and large (100+100) pair counts.
Table~\ref{tab:run-configs} summarizes configurations.

\section*{Conclusion}

Causal interventions on scGPT token representations enrich for curated
TF--target edges, particularly in lung, and mechanistic tracing localizes
components for specific pairs. The strongest result is lung tissue AUPR up to
0.78 against DoRothEA references with permutation $p{=}0.001$, a signal
that survives across pair counts, robustness runs, and high-confidence
reference subsets. Kidney enrichment is modest but reproducible across seeds,
and immune tissue remains at baseline, together delineating where the model
has and has not learned directional regulatory structure. A matched
benchmark against eleven classical GRN inference methods spanning six
algorithmic families confirms that scGPT leads the strongest classical
baseline in all three tissues, with the lung gap substantially larger
than the kidney or immune gaps, consistent with the tissue-level
pattern of the main runs.

Several directions follow naturally from these findings. First, scaling runs
to GPU-enabled full-tissue evaluations would increase pair counts by an order
of magnitude, sharpening the statistical resolution for tissues where current
enrichment is marginal. Second, replacing the mean-across-features readout
with richer target representations, such as per-feature cosine similarity or
learned probes, could recover regulatory effects that a single scalar
compresses away. Third, applying the same causal intervention framework to
other single-cell foundation models (Geneformer, scFoundation) would reveal
whether tissue-dependent regulatory encoding is architecture-specific or a
general property of expression-trained transformers. Finally, perturbation
validation using tissue-matched Perturb-seq datasets, particularly from lung
epithelial cells where model performance is strongest, would close the gap
between representational causal effects and experimental ground truth that the
Adamson K562 comparison currently leaves open.

More broadly, the pipeline we describe, combining token-level causal
interventions with component-level activation patching, provides a general
template for mechanistic audits of biological foundation models. As these
models are increasingly deployed for cell-type annotation, perturbation
prediction, and drug target prioritization, understanding which internal
components encode which biological relationships becomes a practical safety
and reliability concern, not merely an academic curiosity. The artifacts and
evaluation framework accompanying this work are designed to lower the barrier
for such audits across tissues, models, and regulatory contexts.
